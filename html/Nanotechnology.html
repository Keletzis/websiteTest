Nanotechnology

Fig. 1 Representation of the microcosm [1].

Definition

According to Collins dictionary [1] nanotechnology is “a branch of technology dealing with the manufacture of objects with dimensions of less than 100 nanometres and the manipulation of individual molecules and atoms”.

History of Nanotechnology

Even though, exposure to nanoparticles have been a part of human life throughout history, it greatly inflated amid the industrial revolution. Richard Zsigmondy, the 1925 Nobel Prize Laureate in chemistry, was the first to compute the size of particles and present the notion of nanometre. He also linked this term specifically for characterizing particle size[2].

However, present day nanotechnology was the brain child of Richard Feynman, the 1965 Nobel Prize Laureate in physics. In the course of his lecture titled “There’s Plenty of Room at the Bottom”, in the 1959 American Physical Society meeting at Caltech, he was the first to present the idea of utilizing matter at the atomic level[2].

  • Fig. 3 Richard Feynman [3].

The term “nanotechnology” was introduced by a Japanese scientist, Norio Taniguchi, in order to classify semiconductor operations that take place on the order of the nanometre[2]. In the 1980s, an age of prosperity began for nanotechnology when “Kroto, Smalley, and Curl discovered fullerenes and Eric Drexler of Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) used ideas from Feynman’s lecture and Taniguchi’s term nanotechnology in his 1986 book” titled, “Engines of Creation: The Coming Era of Nanotechnology”[2]. Drexler suggested the notion of “nanoscale assembler which would be able to build a copy of itself and of other items of arbitrary complexity”[2]. Afterwards, nanotechnology continued to grow when Iijima developed carbon nanotubes.

Nowadays, nanotechnology has become the bedrock for exponential growth of other technologies such as engineering, medicine, electronics and many more[2].

General Information

The benefits of nanotechnology derive from the fact that it allows the creation of a large array of substances with beneficial implementations from an abundance of atoms and molecules. It basically, allows the invention and realisation of the materials needed in order to generate the necessary products by changing the position of atoms. This, in turn, allows the development of materials with elevated sturdiness, less weight and other desirable aspects[3].

Due to these, nanotechnology can be used in a great variety of fields. For instance, in the pharmaceutical community, it has greatly effected medical devices such as diagnostic biosensors, drug delivery systems, and imaging probes[2]. “In the food and cosmetics industries, use of nanomaterials has increased dramatically for improvements in production, packaging, shelf life, and bioavailability”[2].

Fig. 6 Nanoscience in multyple fields.[6].

For this reason, in the past few years nanotechnology is being considered as a huge leap towards the future in terms of technological advancement and it has also enticed the interest of organisations from all around the world for its projected economic benefits[3].

Since it can be used into multiple fields, it has brought about the creation of a great number of job prospects. It may also instigate an affluence of limited substances such as wood or oil because of its synthesising power[4]. However, it has also lead to the need of collaboration of personnel from various departments which can sometimes be seen as a negative aspect in the creation process of a product since it adds to its complexity factor[4]. Another negative prospect is that nanotechnology may extend the gap between the wealthy and the underprivileged.
